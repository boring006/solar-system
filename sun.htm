The Sun

The Living Heart of Our Solar System

About Our Star

What is the Sun?

The Sun is a glowing sphere of plasma held together by its own gravity. It generates energy through nuclear fusion in its core, releasing light and heat that sustain life on Earth.

Composition

The Sun is composed primarily of hydrogen (about 74% of its mass) and helium (about 24%), with heavier elements making up the remaining 2%.

Did You Know?

Light from the Sun takes only 8 minutes to reach Earth. The Sun contains 99.8% of all mass in the Solar System!

Structure of the Sun

The Sun is composed of several distinct layers:

  • Core : where nuclear fusion occurs, producing immense energy.
  • Radiative Zone : energy slowly travels outward over thousands of years.
  • Convective Zone : hot plasma rises and sinks, creating motion.
  • Photosphere : the visible “surface” of the Sun.
  • Chromosphere : a reddish layer seen during eclipses.
  • Corona : the Sun’s outer atmosphere, hotter than the surface.

Energy & Fusion

Every second, the Sun fuses 600 million tons of hydrogen into helium. This releases energy equivalent to billions of nuclear bombs.

This energy powers the Solar System, controls climates, and drives every biological process on Earth.

Solar Phenomena

  • Sunspots : cooler, darker regions caused by magnetic activity.
  • Solar Flares : explosive bursts of radiation.
  • Coronal Mass Ejections : massive plasma eruptions.
  • Solar Wind : high-speed particles that interact with Earth’s magnetic field, creating auroras.

Solar Timeline

4.6 Billion Years Ago

The Sun forms from the gravitational collapse of a region within a large molecular cloud.

4.5 Billion Years Ago

Nuclear fusion ignites in the Sun's core, marking the beginning of its main sequence phase.

Present Day

The Sun is about halfway through its main sequence lifetime, steadily fusing hydrogen into helium.

5 Billion Years From Now

The Sun will exhaust the hydrogen in its core and begin to expand into a red giant.
